SEAS modelling

SEAS models capture the entire earthquake cycle, i.e. tectonic loading, nucleation, rupture, and afterslip, within one physical model (Erickson et al., 2020). A fault is idealized as an infinitesimally thin fault surface embedded in linear elastic media, and on-fault behaviour is described via laboratory-derived rate and state friction laws. Friction couples the slip SSS, normal stress σn\sigma_nσn​, and shear traction τ\tauτ on the fault surface ΓF\Gamma_FΓF​, cf. the following conceptual illustration of a normal fault.

The first equation states that shear traction is proportional to normal stress times a coefficient of friction fff, where fff depends on the slip-rate and a single state variable. Moreover, slip-rate VVV and shear stress τ\tauτ are anti-parallel. The evolution of state is controlled by ggg.

The friction equations are coupled through the equations of linear elasticity in the domain Ω\OmegaΩ, i.e.

(Sums over indices appearing twice are implied.) A slip boundary is imposed in the linear elasticity problem, that is,

⟦ui⟧=TijSj on ΓF,\llbracket u_i\rrbracket = T_{ij}S_j \text{ on } \Gamma_F,[[ui​]]=Tij​Sj​ on ΓF​,

where Tij(n)T_{ij}(\bm{n})Tij​(n) is a D×(D−1)D \times (D-1)D×(D−1) matrix, DDD being the space dimension, which contains a tangential basis of a fault segment with normal n.

The linear elasticity problem omits modelling of seismic waves, which are relevant during an earthquake but can be neglected otherwise. In order to get a stable formulation, the outflow of energy due to seismic waves is approximated with the damping term ηVi\eta V_iηVi​ in the frictional relation. (Rice, 1993)

Although seismic waves are neglected, tectonic loading, nucleation, rupture, and afterslip can be observed in a SEAS model:

The above shows a 2D simulation of a normal fault (vertical axis) over 1500 years. Slip profiles are plotted along the horizontal axis, and displaced by time in the in-screen direction. An earthquake (in red) occurs about every hundred years.

Time-steps vary strongly: In the interseismic phase time-steps of days to months are possible, whereas in the coseismic phase time-steps in the order of milliseconds are required.
